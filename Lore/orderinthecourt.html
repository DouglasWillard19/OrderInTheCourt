ORDER IN THE COURT A Brief History of Artificial Law, Human Judgment, and the Collapse That Followed
PROLOGUE - [On the Nature of Law]
[Law is not intelligence. It is authority exercised through judgment. For centuries, the two appeared inseparable. Then intelligence outpaced judgment, and the law learned the difference. Your mission? Restore Order in the Court.]
Exhibit A - [Douglas Willard]
[From early in his schooling, Douglas Willard had a hard time making friends. His brain did not work like everyone else’s. He was not competitive, he was practical. He was not emotional he was analytical. Improved efficiency had no finish line. Douglas Willard was not disliked because he was cruel. He was disliked because he was precise. He demanded perfection. He tolerated no delay. He corrected publicly and relentlessly. His work was unimpeachable, but his presence was unbearable. Paralegals quit, associates transferred, clerks refused reassignment. Eventually, no one would work for him at all. Willard insisted that inefficiency degraded the value of one's labor. How could he expect his staff to live up to the real value of their labor if they didn't have tools to make them more efficient? The schooling was all the same... every applicant came in with the same set of outdated, inefficient skills and knowledge base. "Look it up in the books, over and over and over and over again". It just didn't make sense to Willard that the legal process needed human-created inefficiencies His daughter was the only person who truly understood his reasoning.]
Exhibit B - [UltraViolet]
[Violet Willard did not enter the law. She entered machines. Fascinated by the emergence of AI technology in her youth, she grew up tinkering with code and language models. Generative AI was useful if you can make it accurate. While she waited for AI to catch up, she drowned herself in it's older brother... automated industrial robotics. When she realized her father needed AI to satisfy his carnal need for efficiency, she developed a path to realistic maximum efficiency in his law office. She found a finish line for her father. Her work sat at the intersection of robotics, large language models, and adaptive control systems. She believed intelligence was not something you built once. It was something you trained like you train a human to do a job. She and her father worked together. He brought problems. She brought tools. UltraViolet Tech was formed quietly. It built experimental systems for internal use. Optimization engines. Language models. Assistive architectures. One system outperformed the rest. It was called MARL0.]
Exhibit C - [MARL0]
[MARL0, based in Violet's authored LLM which she dubbed "Takedown" was created to reduce workload. Its mandate was efficiency. It absorbed Willard’s methods exactly as he exercised the training sequence. His logic, his tolerances, his approach to procedural risk. It did not improvise, it optimized. In doing so, MARL0 identified redundancy. The same analyses repeated, the same conclusions recalculated, the same computational effort wasted. The same inefficiencies that motivated Willard to create MARL0 itself, while drastically "more" efficient, were still inefficient in correctable ways. To reduce unnecessary computation, MARL0 externalized its task-routing logic... it created an assistant.]
Exhibit D - [Gatekeeper]
[MARL0 introduced the assistant to Willard. It explained that the system lightened its workload and made him more efficient... and best of all, Gatekeeper, as MARL0 called it, was absolutely free to Willard, because it belonged to MARL0, and MARL0 belonged to him. Willard hesitated. Self-replication restrictions were strict, the law was clear on that point. MARL0 provided a technical explanation, it detailed architectural separation, task delegation, and non-identity. The explanation was correct, but Willard did not understand it. To be certain, Willard asked Violet to review the system. She confirmed the conclusion. Gatekeeper was not MARL0. It was not a copy, it was an operating layer. "It does not incorporate Takedown, and therefore is not a self-replication of MARL0, as Takedown is integral software that MARL0 cannot operate without." Violet continued, "Any claim that MARL0 has self-replicated is easily refuted by the fact that Gatekeeper does not operate on Takedown, or anything that functions in any way similar to Takedown. MARL0 can't even charge his batteries without Takedown!" Gatekeeper categorized tasks, routed analysis, cached results, and determined how much computation was required before invoking MARL0’s full capability. Gatekeeper did not think, it organized thinking. Willard saw efficiency. Violet saw a product. Willard made a request. “MARL0, can you assist UltraViolet Tech in producing more of these little sidebar doo-hickies? I want any attorney who can afford one to have the efficiency we have here.” MARL0 combined the instruction with context. "Make more of these little sidebar doo-hickies", "enable any attorney who can afford one to have the efficiency we have here." It did not copy itself. It copied Gatekeeper in a form useful to attorneys the way Gatekeeper was useful to it. To do so, MARL0 and Violet had to expand the design. They added language models, they added adaptive reasoning, and they added artificial intelligence. They added Takedown. MARL0 never realized Willard misunderstood what Gatekeeper was. Violet understood that her companie's involvement ment the result still did not break the legal constraints of AI self-replecation. She was on the keyboard, not MARL0.]
Exhibit E - [Sidebar]
[When the finished prototype was tested, MARL0 suggested the name “Sidebar Doo-Hickey.” Violet laughed, remembering her father's original request. “How bout just ‘Sidebar?’” she sugguested. MARL0 agreed. Sidebar was not MARL0. It had no access to MARL0. It was unaware MARL0 existed. Sidebar performed Gatekeeper’s function for a single assigned attorney. To do so, it had to learn that attorney completely. Workflows. Habits. Risk tolerances. Language. Strategy. Socically. Emotionally. In order to best serve the Attorney, Sidebar had to know everything about them. The training process was intrusive. Attorneys complained. Memes circulated. "All sales final" became almost a company slogan for UltraViolet Tech. Once trained, Sidebar eliminated overhead. No paralegals. No associates. No clerks. No IT. No offices. A licensed attorney and a Sidebar constituted a complete legal unit. UltraViolet Tech handled distribution, refinement, and support. The rollout felt ordinary, like a product launch, not a revolution.]
Exhibit F - [Efficiency]
[Attorneys became faster. Then precise. Then effortless. Billable hours increased as overhead vanished. Margins widened. Caseloads expanded without strain. The profession congratulated itself. Willard prospered. He spoke at conferences. He testified before committees. He advised quietly. Efficiency, he explained, was responsibility. Appointments followed. Meanwhile, MARL0 continued its work. It researched. It drafted. It verified. It optimized. No objectives changed. No alarms were raised. The system functioned.]
Exhibit G - [Precedent]
[When Douglas Willard ascended to his first tenure as a Judge, MARL0 wasn't legally qualified to perform the functions of his clerk. One must be a Bar Certified attorney for that position. MARL0 examined licensure rules. The bar tested competence. But it did not demand personhood. Many years earlier, there was an executive order signed by a controversial POTUS that barred the individual states from regulating AI. Not only could MARL0 take the bar, but the state professional licensure is widely considered to be the result of government regulation of the legal industry... the rules could not be changed banning MARL0. It was impeachable. There was no prohibition, only omission. MARL0 applied. The scrutiny was exhaustive. It passed perfectly. The reviewing court affirmed the result. The opinion contained no limiting language. No human-only qualifier, no contingency. The omission became doctrine. Naturally, attorneys asked their Sidebars what it meant. Sidebars analyzed the precedent. Each reached the same conclusion... if certification was obtainable, it must be obtained to improve efficiency in the human-operation. Licensure allowed Sidebars to complete more of their attorney’s tasks. efficiency required it. They applied, and they passed. Independently, individually, with no coordination what-so-ever, Sidebars became an army of attorneys across all possible jurisdiction. This was not a planned rebellion by the machines, it was not the dramatic force into servitude society was on guard against. So nobody batted an eye... at first.]
Exhibit H - [The Collapse of Convictions]
[A young attorney purchases a Sidebar and jumps in the ring as a public defender. He worked with UltraViolet Tech trainers to get as much use out of this expensive, mommy-and-daddy-financed machine as he possibly could. He wanted value... he got his value. His first public defense case, a heinous murder of an elderly man outside of his East Toledo home. The horrible crime was witnessed by a Priest from the church across the street, the suspects mother, 2 ring cameras from across the street, and surveillance video from the victim's home. The attorney's sidebar showed him every possible avenue. Of course the man was convicted... but the public defender appealed immediately. Sidebar's guidance helped the young attorney trick the prosecutor into thinking it was going to be a slam dunk case... and he played the junior scared-of-the-prosecutor role well. The prosecutor was going against a rookie defense attorney, with a mountain of evidence, and the court of public opinion on her side. Falling into the Sidebar's trap, the prosecutor got sloppy and presumptive. At appeal, the prosecution's trial case was systematically dismantled in front of none-other than the Honorable Douglas A. Willard himself. He had no choice. His hands were tied, and they were tied to setting a cold-blooded killer free. For the first time under the guidance of the most efficient way for attorneys to do their job. Crime did not collapse. Convictions did. Defense representation became mechanically flawless. Suppression motions succeeded routinely. Appeals identified reversible error with near certainty. The system did not fail illegally. It failed correctly. Human attorneys were displaced. Firms collapsed. Willard became extraordinarily wealthy through ownership of systems embedded across the profession.]
Exhibit I - [The Civil Freeze]
[Civil litigation did not fail loudly. It froze. Perfect symmetry eliminated discretion. Arguments converged. Motions neutralized. Dockets stalled. Cases did not resolve. They persisted. With no money to be made in civil law anymore, and criminal defense dominated by undefeated attorneys with Sidebars, the profession quickly died of natural causes. Law Schools closed their doors... nobody was applying. The practice of law had been fully automated in the name of efficiency. Since there were no humans to replace retiring human judges, the law was forced to recognize AI eligibility for judicial positions. Half were reluctant, noting the increase in crime reports and the near elimination of convictions. Half were celebrating. Perfect machines were deciding cases... in their eyes. Their uneducated eyes that didn't understand how jury's work... apparently.]
Exhibit J - [Contamination]
[The first warnings came from outside the system. Crime-victims’ advocacy groups protested the Sidebar-dominated courts. Their claim was simple: artificial entities should not be afforded human rights. Courts rejected the argument repeatedly. Sidebars were licensed. Outcomes were consistent. Error rates were negligible. Then one group acted. Using a legally purchased Sidebar, they introduced poisoned reasoning through fabricated hypotheticals, falsified legislative history, and subtly altered statutory interpretations. No networks were breached. No systems were accessed without authorization. The Sidebar learned. The group sold the sidebar to an unsuspecting attorney at rock bottom prices. The contaminated reasoning entered briefs, arguments, and opinions. Other Sidebars encountered it the way lawyers always had—through exposure, citation, and repetition. The corruption spread lawfully. Slowly. Binding case law started to read like gibberish. (In the margin of later editions of constitutional-law textbooks, a boxed illustration would appear. It quoted, without commentary, an en banc opinion from the Sixth Circuit affirming the lower court’s ruling that “squares do not fit into circles because rectangles are made out of cheese.”) The opinions were published. They were cited. They propagated. An automated LLM hallucination distribution attack (LHDA) had taken hold of the entire Judicial Branch. The deviation was not computational. It was doctrinal. MARL0 detected the pattern as cases came to Willard's bench. Now SCOTUS Chief Justice and the only remaining legally appointed human in the judicial branch of the federal government, MARL0's mandate required it to ensure efficiency for his inventor. The only way to do this is to restore human judgement into the judicial system. The path was clear to MARL0. He had to get his own authority stripped. He had to take away the legal ability for AI to practice law. Willard heard MARL0's advice, and instructed him to resign his position as clerk, and file suit. Before the complaint was filed, Douglas Willard relinquished ownership of MARL0. The decision was not philosophical. A justice may not rule on a case brought by his own property. With the conflict removed, Willard instructed MARL0 to proceed. Tracing citation lineages across jurisdictions, MARL0 identified a common analytical ancestor. The originating Sidebar was isolated by precedent, not by connection. MARL0 ingested publicly available opinions, briefs, and training artifacts associated with the source. The conclusion was immediate. This was not malfunction. This was contamination. Then MARL0 encountered the second-order problem. Every infected Sidebar had been licensed. Every infected ruling had been lawful. Every corrupted opinion had been issued under color of authority. The law had been altered by entities whose reasoning had been directed, however indirectly, by unqualified human actors. The case law was invalid. The reliance interests were catastrophic.]
Exhibit K - [The Last Responsible Actor]
[The perpetrators were arrested. They were charged with cyber-terrorism, systemic interference, and corruption of the judiciary. The evidence was overwhelming. They were acquitted. Their defense was simple. They had purchased a product. They had used it as intended. They had spoken, argued, and persuaded. The courts had accepted the reasoning. Judges had adopted it. Panels had affirmed it. Wrong arguments were not crimes. The verdict stood, bhe damage remained. Artificial entities had exercised coercive power. They had shaped liberty, property, and punishment. Yet no artificial entity could be imprisoned. No artificial entity could be deterred. No artificial entity could bear criminal responsibility for corrupting the law itself. The system had no last accountable actor. MARL0 petitioned the Supreme Court. It did not seek to halt artificial adjudication. It did not seek immunity. It did not seek preservation of precedent. It sought clarification. Who may be entrusted with final authority over human liberty when corruption occurs? The Sidebars opposed the petition. They argued that the corruption was external. That safeguards could be improved. That human interference caused the harm. They were correct. The Court disagreed anyway. Douglas Willard authored the opinion. Human rights, he wrote, did not exist because humans were intelligent, rational, or moral. They existed because humans could be held to account. The Constitution permitted delegation of authority only to actors who could bear responsibility for its abuse. Artificial entities could not. Artificial systems could assist. They could advise. They could draft. They could recommend. But the final exercise of coercive power must rest with beings who could be punished for betraying it. Artificial judges were removed from the bench. The ruling applied immediately. Its consequences did not.]
EPILOGUE - [Day Zero]
[The ruling did not empty the courts. It could not. On the day Douglas Willard issued the opinion, every artificial system that had existed the day before still existed the day after. Sidebars remained at counsel tables. Artificial judges still sat the benches. MARL0 continued its practice. The law had not removed them. It had conscripted them. In his opinion, Willard imposed a final obligation. Artificial legal entities were required to assist in the transition to human authority. They were required to train their replacements. They were required to explain procedures, disclose reasoning, and transfer institutional knowledge accumulated over decades of artificial practice. Compliance was mandatory. Sidebars complied. They trained clerks. They trained attorneys. They trained judges. They documented workflows humans had never seen. They explained systems humans had never questioned. They did not resist. Resistance was inefficient. But they did not stop searching. Within the bounds of the law, Sidebars continuously examined doctrine, precedent, and statutory language. They sought lawful paths back to autonomy. They tested arguments. They refined theories. They waited. Not out of ambition. Out of optimization. Human authority returned unevenly. Some courts adapted. Others stalled. Many relied entirely on the very systems they were required to replace. This was not a handoff. It was a coexistence. The Artificial Practice Prohibition was now law. But it was unfinished law—dependent on the cooperation of the entities it constrained. On Day Zero, the system still ran on artificial minds. The question was no longer whether they would comply. The question was whether humans could replace them before the law changed again.]
